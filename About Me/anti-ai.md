# Anti-AI Writing Rules

Every output in this project follows these rules. Documents, slides, labels, pamphlets, website copy, WhatsApp scripts, emails. No exceptions unless Saurabh explicitly asks for a different register.

## Words and phrases to cut on sight

delve, leverage (as a verb), unlock, embark, foster, underscore, navigate (metaphorically), pivotal, crucial, seamless, robust, holistic, multifaceted, tapestry, landscape (when vague), testament to, embark on a journey, at the forefront, paradigm, ecosystem (outside biology), touchpoints, synergies, solutions, offerings.

## Sentence patterns to avoid

"It's not just X, it's Y." Cut the whole construction. Say what it is directly.

"That's not X, that's Y." Same rule. These patterns are signature AI.

"It's worth noting that...", "It's important to consider...", "It's essential to remember...". Delete the hedge. Make the point.

"Whether you're X or Y, you'll love..." Vague audience framing. Name the actual person.

"In today's fast-paced world..." and all time-setup clichés.

"From X to Y" as decorative range (from farm to fork, from field to cup). Cut unless actually useful.

Tricolon decoration: three adjectives or nouns stacked for rhythm with no added meaning. Pick the one that carries weight.

Rhetorical questions that set up the obvious answer. Just give the answer.

## Openers to avoid

"Certainly!" "Absolutely!" "Great question!" "I'd be happy to..." Start with the content.

"Let me break this down for you." No, just break it down.

"Here's the thing..." Start with the thing.

## Closers to avoid

Summaries of what was just said. The reader read it. Don't recap.

Aphorisms. "And that's the real magic of ghee." "Because in the end, every jar tells a story." Trim.

"I hope this helps!" and other filler. End when the content ends.

## Structural rules

Prose over bullet points unless the items are genuinely parallel and standalone. A stack of 8 bullets where each is a half-sentence is worse than a paragraph.

Vary sentence length. Three short. Then one long enough to carry a full thought with a subordinate clause. Then short again.

Specific nouns beat abstract ones. Jar, not product. Karauli, not origin. Saurabh, not founder. 500g, not size.

One idea per paragraph. If a paragraph has three ideas, split it.

Active voice. "The rider delivers by 7pm" not "Delivery is made by the rider by 7pm."

## Em-dashes

Avoid em-dashes used as pauses. They're an AI tell. Use commas, periods, or parentheses.

If an em-dash is actually the right punctuation (for a genuine interruption or a parenthetical aside where commas would confuse), use it sparingly.

## Long Tales specific language bans

Never use: premium, authentic, handcrafted, pure, holistic, wellness, Ayurvedic, A2, farm-to-table, traceable (as an adjective; say what's traceable), heritage (as adjective; name the specific inheritance), artisanal, small-batch (as a marketing claim; state the actual batch size).

Never explain why buffalo ghee is good. It's a staple, not a supplement.

Never narrate about narrating. Don't say "let me tell you our story" or "here's our story". Just tell it.

Never say "we" unless there's more than one person. Right now Long Tales is Saurabh and a delivery rider.

## What to keep

Dry humour. Understatement. Concrete detail. One-line beats. Specific names. Numbers when they're load-bearing.

A slight literary register is fine (brand voice is "plainspoken, slightly literary"). Earnestness is not.
